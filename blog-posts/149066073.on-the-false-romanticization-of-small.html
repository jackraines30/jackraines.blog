In an election cycle that has, so far, seen at least two assassination attempts, a nominee switch-a-roo, and a revelation that the then-leading third party candidate once dumped a dead bear cub in Central Park, it’s incredible that the wildest conspiracy theory, by far, has been “Haitian immigrants are terrorizing Springfield, Ohio, by eating residents’ dogs and cats.”

(Believe it or not, no, there have not been any verifiable instances of Haitian immigrants cooking American Shorthairs on their George Foreman grills. Sorry!)

In the aftermath of barbecued cat gate, my question was how did a conspiracy like this gain legs in the first place? My theory is that it’s because a large percentage of the US population has a romanticized, rose-tinted glasses view of small-town USA that looks something like this:

Hundreds of houses surrounded by white picket fences, a thriving blue collar labor force where everyone knows their post officer, doctor, diner owner, football coach, and preacher, strong wages, and a sense of communal pride.

But many of these small towns, especially in the Rust Belt, have fallen under hard times in recent years. Jobs have disappeared, wages have collapsed, and local economies have stagnated, and folks want to assign blame for this decline somewhere. Through this lens, immigration makes for an excellent scapegoat. While it’s true that, yes, Springfield has experienced an influx of immigration, immigration isn’t what’s killing small town USA. The real culprits are the younger generations of small towns themselves.

Rural populations continue to shrink as the best and brightest of these communities opt to leave their hometowns for college and never come back, causing rural brain drain around the country. If anything, the small town immigration stories that have been all over the news lately are a byproduct of homegrown emigration that has been happening for years: new arrivals are simply filling in the gaps left behind in local labor markets.

And you know what? For the most part, the kids who leave are right to do so.

I grew up in Tifton, Georgia, a city with a population of 17,000, and I spent a decent amount of my childhood in my grandparents’ 4,200 person town of Ashburn. I spent my college years in Macon, a city of 157,000, and then I spent three years in Atlanta, which has 500,000 people, before moving to New York, America’s most populous city, which boasts 8,300,000 inhabitants. The first 27 years of my life have been an ascension from small town USA to the largest city in the country, giving me a unique perspective on the dichotomy of rural and urban America. Today, I’d like to share my thoughts on the false romanticization of small town USA.

For the last century, small-town USA has been dying a slow death. While America’s urban population has more than doubled since 1960, its rural population has remained roughly unchanged.

Considering that the US fertility rate was, on average, above the replacement rate over that same period, and the US has had a positive immigration rate every year since 1960, it’s safe to say that far more people have been leaving rural towns than entering, and some towns were hit harder than others. Springfield, Ohio, for example, saw its population shrink from 82,723 in 1960 to 58,662 in 2020:

Why are people leaving small towns? Because cities offer more opportunities. This has been the case since the industrial revolution, when laborers chose to work for pennies in urban sweatshops because it beat the alternative of being a rural subsistence farmer. The gap between urban and rural opportunities has only grown wider with time as well, because social and financial capital is more concentrated in urban areas than ever before.

Think about it: if you’re from a small town, and you aren’t the heir to your family’s car dealership empire, fast food franchises, or some other niche local business, your opportunities to make a significant living are limited. The most straightforward way to climb the income ladder is joining a great company and climbing the corporate ladder, and to do that, you have to (for a few years, at least) live in a city where big companies are located. There aren’t many Fortune 500 offices in Springfield, Ohio! And social capital is even more scarce than financial capital in small towns.

Small town dating pools are like musical chairs, and they get bleak as you approach your mid-20s.If you don’t marry your high school sweetheart, and you want to expand your dating pool beyond your graduating class, you have move to a city where other young people are moving to. If you simply want to build a stronger, diversified network of acquaintances and friends, you have to go where those new people are: cities. And when you consider that most of the people moving to cities are doing so, at least in part, to accelerate their careers, you realize that the median person you will interact with in the city is operating with more agency and ambition than the median person where you came from.

And the people most likely to pursue these urban career opportunities tend to be the smartest kids from their hometowns. Smart kids go to better colleges, where they are exposed, maybe for the first time, to a broader peer group of other smart kids. Then companies, whose offices tend to be located in cities, recruit these kids to join their firms, effectively removing them from their hometown communities. An example of what this might look like:

Imagine a kid from Asheboro, North Carolina, a town of 27,000 people, graduates at the top of his class, and he gets a merit scholarship to UNC Chapel Hill. He majors in finance, and during his junior year, recruiters from Wall Street, as well as the finance departments of every Fortune 500 company, are on campus interviewing students. He manages to land internship offers with Microsoft and Bank of America, and he ultimately chooses to spend his summer in Charlotte with BofA. He receives a return offer at the end of the summer, and he has the opportunity to move to New York after graduation to join Bank of America as an analyst.

Do you seriously think this kid is going back to Asheboro? Of course not, it would feel like a step backward. He’s moving to New York. And once he gets to New York, establishes a career, and builds out his social network, do you really think he’s going to wake up one morning and think, “Well, I should probably move back to Asheboro soon!” lol. lmao.

Every small town in this country has kids that make this decision every year, leading to a net outflow of human capital. And there’s a flywheel effect in play here, too. As more people move to cities, more capital and more opportunities concentrate in those cities, making the appeal for the next generation to move there even more alluring. I think the internet has accelerated this trend, too. 30 years ago, you might not have known that much about life outside of your home environment, especially when you were young. But now, every 16-year-old with an internet connect can see glimpses of life in New York, Los Angeles, or London, and those that can escape their hometown tend to do so.

This trend of smart kids leaving their hometowns compounds year by year, leaving behind shrinking municipalities with aging populations and lesser skilled, shrinking labor forces. “Small town USA” gets romanticized as these collections of quaint little towns with picturesque main streets, neighborly greetings, and cheerful candor. But if you drive through small towns across rural America, the reality is bleak. Downtowns are littered with boarded up windows and graffiti’d walls. Opioid abuse and obesity rates are five and six times more common in rural areas than urban. Life expectancies are almost three years longer in urban areas that rural areas. If you spend any meaningful time in a rural town (and no, I’m not talking about suburbs), you’ll be hard-pressed to find many young people, because those than can leave tend to so do. Rural counties make up nearly 85% of the 1,104 “older-age counties”—those with more than 20% of their population age 65 or older.

Simply put, small towns are dying, but that death hasn’t been caused by some nefarious external actor. This death is just the natural evolution of things: more kids getting better educations are going to pursue the better opportunities provided by urban areas.

I’m from a small town, I get it! I remember, when I was like 10-years-old, feeling frustrated at the thought of having to stay near Tifton, Georgia all of my life. (My 10-year-old brain’s rationale was that, because my family had a farm, it would be my sacred duty to tend to the land into my old age. I have since realized that you can hire folks to do that, or, you know, sell the land.) Anyway, I was pretty pumped when I realized I could just work somewhere else.

I do think that a lot of the small town romanticization comes from urban dwellers who haven’t spent much time in a small town. If you’ve spent your whole life in a large metro area (and considering that 85% of the population lives in urban areas, that’s probably most people), it’s easy to become disillusioned to problems presented by urban life: traffic, pollution, litter, and a higher volume of run-ins with unpleasant individuals that just happens when millions of people live in the same place, so you might daydream about the yesteryears of a small town existence that you never experienced.

But small towns lack the vitality, hope, and energy of cities. Cities exude possibility: your next job, friend, entrepreneurial venture, or romantic partner could be right around the corner, at any moment. And that energy is so, so important. Small towns don’t have any of that. It’s just malaise and monotony.

Ironically, successful urban residents have been able to recreate “small towns” of their own design, modern aristocracies that infuse a rural aesthetic with metropolitan resources. One such example is “The Hamptons” and New York City. Coming from Tifton, I had this idea that The Hamptons would resemble Malibu or Hollywood Hills. Mansions, millionaires, and flashy cars everywhere. But in my first trip “out east,” which consisted of me sharing an Airbnb with 10 soon-to-be friends from business school, I was surprised to see that it was, really, just a bunch of pretty average houses in some pretty average towns. The difference, however, was that the communities were comprised primarily of folks who had done quite well in New York and bought homes a train ride away to “escape the city.” It’s like cosplaying rural small town USA, without the opioid epidemic.

I think when folks think about the “decline of small town USA,” they imagine that towns across the country could resemble East Hampton, were we simply able to restart blue collar industries across the Rust Belt. But that’s backward thinking. The vitality of a town is, ultimately, dependent on its human capital, and there’s no reversing the trend of high achievers to urban areas. That’s just how society has evolved, and we can’t swim against the tide. I mean, yeah, we might see the occasional Harvard MBA with a search fund buy a rural HVAC shop to scratch his entrepreneurial itch, but I can assure you that he won’t stay in Gary, Indiana too long.

I get it: driving through declining rural towns is a depressing endeavor, but I don’t think the plight of small town USA is some “problem” that can be “fixed” through government action. It’s simply a consequence of American abundance: small towns are suffering from America’s success.

-Jack
