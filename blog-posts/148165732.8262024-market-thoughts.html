As mentioned in my last piece on markets, I decided to begin recording my thoughts on markets and specific stocks in my blog. I made a few investments last week, so I wanted to timestamp my reasoning for those investments. My thoughts:

I don’t think that the S&P 500 is due for a massive correction, I do think there are some better opportunities than the Mag-7 (which is now 31% of the index). Three trends that I have focused on over the last few weeks are:

  • China is trading at an all-time discount to US tech, valuation-wise.

  • Several companies that went public in 2020/2021 sold off due to high valuations at IPO.

  • Several companies’ valuations got extended in 2021, but they’re reasonable again.

From those observations, I identified the following opportunities:

Alibaba (BABA)

I took a position in Alibaba last week. The way-too-simplified reason is the valuation gap between US and China stocks is at an all-time high, Alibaba is the Chinese market leader in both e-commerce and cloud, it’s investing heavily in building out its cloud infrastructure, its price to forward earnings and price to cash flow ratios are near all-time lows, and the company is buying back billions of dollars of stock and issuing dividends.

For context, here are the stock charts for Alibaba and the KWEB (China Internet) ETF over the last 10 years:

Chinese tech sold off from Q1 2021 until it bottomed in October 2022, and the sector has spent the last ~2 years trading around 20% or so off the lows. While the stocks have suffered, many businesses have been doing just fine. BABA’s revenue is up 10x and earnings have more than tripled since 2014. So why am I investing now? A few reasons:

  1. I believe that China stocks are long overdue for valuation expansion.

  2. BABA, specifically, is poised to be the biggest winner of said valuation expansion thanks to its dominant position in cloud and e-commerce, its cash position, and its buybacks.

  3. BABA has a near-term catalyst (up-listing on the Hong Kong exchange) that could kickstart momentum.

  4. At its current valuation, much of the risk of owning “China” has been derisked. The biggest issue here, if the trade “doesn’t work,” is opportunity cost: the stock might not go up while the S&P 500 continues to climb. The actual downside risk, however, looks minimal.

Markets are cyclical: investors couldn’t get enough China in 2018 and 2020, then they threw out the baby with the bathwater in 2021 and 2022, and I think soon we’ll seen money return to this sector. No, Chinese companies won’t trade in line with their US peers, but they don’t have to. With US big tech reaching pricy valuations, I think we’ll see investors begin to put money to work in other sectors. China is an attractive option.

BABA is the largest e-commerce player in China, its international e-commerce segment is growing at 30%+ year-over-year, and it dominates the Chinese cloud market. While the Chinese market is behind the US in AI development (largely due to western trade restrictions), we’re beginning to see AI adoption ramp: BABA noted a 200% quarter-over-quarter uptick in paying users on its Cloud AI platform, and the company is investing aggressively in its cloud. If Chinese AI adoption and investment tracks anything like what we’ve seen in the US, BABA is well-positioned.

Alibaba shareholders also just approved an uplisting on the Hong Kong exchange, which opens the stock to capital inflows from Chinese investors. Valuation alone is not a thesis for investment, it’s just a measure of upside potential and downside risk. The market today cares about flows and liquidity as much as it does “value.” The Hong Kong listing could provide some much-needed momentum with new money flowing into the stock.

Finally, the downside risk at this level is low. Yes, Chinese stocks could always go lower because China has XYZ geopolitical risks. I get that. However, those are the same critiques that investors have heard since BABA was at $300. At some point, those risks are incorporated into the price. BABA may very well not go up, but it’s still only up ~25% from its all-time lows. The biggest risk, to me, is the opportunity cost. I’m willing to test that. Again: BABA may not go up. But if China does catch a bid, it’s well-positioned to be one of the biggest winners.

A few things that could impact my thesis: China blocking the Hong Kong uplisting, increased aggression in Taiwan, other heavy-handed government intervention that limits Alibaba as a business.

PayPal (PYPL)

I also invested in PayPal last week. PayPal is a classic example of expectations exceeding reality in 2021, and its stock price collapsed as revenue growth slowed in 2022.

Yet, despite the collapse, revenue and earnings are still growing ~8% and ~10% per year, the company is printing free cash flow, and management is using virtually all of its free cash flow to buy back shares, while the stock is still trading at ~11x free cash flow.

PayPal’s new CEO, Alex Chriss, has thus far been excellent (management raised 2024 FY guidance), and PayPal is executing well across its entire business. Its new guest checkout solution, Fastlane, has proven to provide a massive boost in customer retention for clients, Venmo monetization is improving, and PayPal bought back $5 billion in stock last year. Again, valuation isn’t a catalyst for stock price appreciation (PayPal has been cheap for 2-3 years now), but it looks more attractive than ever compared to other tech stocks, and the new CEO has done an excellent job of communicating this story to investors.

Hims (HIMS)

I took a small stake in Hims last week. From a financial perspective, the company is still growing revenue at 50% annually, it has 82% gross margins (though marketing expenses are very, very high, so this is less relevant than it might be with a software company), net loss has shrunk each quarter since it went public, the company is now profitable, there’s no debt on the balance sheet, and, most importantly, operating leverage is increasing because Hims is spending a lower and lower percent of revenue on marketing.

It’s trading at 50x forward earnings, but the company recently raised its forward guidance. But the thing I love most about Hims? Its business model is built on anxiety, and anxiety = low churn customers. Hims sells ED pills, hair loss solutions, and weight loss drugs. If you’re on these medications, you’ll probably stay on these medications, and that’s a powerful business model.

The biggest risk facing Hims, in the short-term, is legal headwinds related to its weight-loss compounds. Eli Lilly announced several lawsuits against the company, and Hunterbrook published a scathing report regarding the quality of its weight loss compounders.

However, as Needham analyst Ryan McDonald noted, Hims has 40% revenue growth and $1 billion in revenue without its weight-loss drugs. Even if you remove that segment, the business is still solid. This is a small position for now as I expect it to be volatile, but it’s going to be a fun stock to watch.

Warby Parker

I am a huge Warby Parker fan, and I actually bought a pair of glasses from one of their stores in Brooklyn. What I didn’t realize at the time was that Warby Parker was publicly traded, and, like most 2021 IPOs, it went public at a really high valuation before subsequently selling off.

However, much like with PayPal, Warby the business has been doing great, while Warby the stock slipped from $50 to $10. Similar to Hims, Warby’s net loss has shrunk each year since it went public, the company is now EBITDA positive, and its EBITDA margins are expanding, with adjusted EBITDA margins up 200 bps year over year. Notably, gross margins, which had previously shrunk, have now expanded for the last two quarters as well. Stock-based compensation dropped year-over-year, and founders are heavily incentivized for the company to do well, as their founders grant pay packages begin to vest when the stock is at $47.75 (current price ~$15).

Cash flow from operating activities more than covers capex (primarily opening new stores), and the company is careful to grow at a sustainable rate vs. chasing supercharged store count expansion at the expense of profitability. Management also raised its full-year guidance in the last earnings call, for $760 million revenue and $72.5 million adjusted EBITDA (not a great metric, but good to compare YoY changes).

Stock-based compensation is still higher than I’d like (14.8% of revenue), but it’s not terrible, and, more importantly, it’s trending in the right direction: down. Per the most recent earnings call, that percentage is expected to drop to 6% by EOY 2024. Also (and this tends to be an underrated part of an investment thesis): their branding is just… good. Ordering online is easy. Going to a store is easy, and the locations are crisp. I’ll buy my next set of glasses from Warby, 100%.

I took a small position last week (fortuitous timing, with JPM initiating coverage as a “buy” the next day.)
